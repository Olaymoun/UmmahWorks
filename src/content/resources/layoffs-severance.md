# Navigating Layoffs and Severance

Being laid off is a business event, not a performance verdict. Companies reduce headcount for financial, strategic, and structural reasons that have nothing to do with individual quality. Knowing exactly what to do in the first 48 hours and how to evaluate what you are owed will determine how quickly you recover and how well you land. Treat this as a procedural problem, not an identity crisis.

---

## The First 48 Hours

You will likely receive very little time to process before you are expected to make decisions. Most companies walk laid-off employees through a termination meeting that includes a separation agreement, a request to sign by a specific date, and an immediate or near-immediate access revocation. Here is exactly what to do.

### Immediate Checklist (Day of Layoff)

**Before your access is revoked:**
- [ ] Download any personal documents you stored in work systems: performance reviews, offer letters, pay stubs, written commendations, promotion letters
- [ ] Export your professional contacts from work email before it is shut down (names and emails to a personal document or address book)
- [ ] Note the phone numbers and personal emails of colleagues you want to stay in touch with
- [ ] Take screenshots of any awards, announcements, or written recognition that demonstrates your accomplishments

**Do not take:**
- Proprietary company data, code, or documents that belong to the company
- Customer lists or confidential business information
- Anything your employment agreement prohibits

**Do not sign anything on the day of the layoff.** In the United States, federal law (the OWBPA for employees over 40) requires a minimum of 21 days to review a separation agreement and 7 days to revoke after signing. Even if you are under 40, you are not legally required to sign immediately. Ask for the full review period regardless of pressure to move faster.

### Within 24 Hours

- [ ] **File for unemployment insurance.** In most states you can file the same day or the next business day. Do not wait. Many states have a waiting period before benefits begin, and the clock starts when you file, not when you are laid off. Search "[your state] unemployment insurance" and go directly to the government website.
- [ ] **Review your health insurance coverage end date.** Most employer-sponsored plans end either on the last day of employment or the last day of the month in which you were laid off. Confirm this with HR in writing.
- [ ] **Do not post publicly about the layoff yet.** Resist the urge to post on LinkedIn or social media while you are processing the emotion of the first 24 hours. Your public statement (see Section 3) should be deliberate, not reactive.
- [ ] **Forward any pending invoices or payment confirmations** if you have personal expenses reimbursable by the company. Submit these before your access is cut off.

### Within 48 Hours

- [ ] Get the separation agreement to a lawyer or at minimum a trusted advisor who has read one before. If you cannot afford a lawyer, many bar associations offer free or low-cost consultations, and many employment lawyers offer a free first call.
- [ ] List every benefit and perk you currently receive: health insurance, dental, vision, FSA/HSA, 401(k) match, stock options, equity grants, commuter benefits, phone stipends. You need to know what you are losing to evaluate what you are owed.
- [ ] Set up a weekly budget immediately. Unemployment benefits replace approximately 40 to 50% of prior income in most states, up to a weekly cap. Do not plan as though your full salary continues.

## Evaluating the Package

Severance packages are negotiable in most cases, and most employees do not negotiate because they are in shock, embarrassed, or unaware they can. The checklist below helps you understand what you are being offered and where there may be room to push.

### Severance Pay

Standard severance is typically one to two weeks per year of service, sometimes capped at a maximum (often 12 to 26 weeks). Executive-level employees often receive more by contract.

Questions to answer about your severance:
- How is it paid out? (Lump sum vs. continuation of regular paychecks) Lump sum is generally preferable because it allows you to receive unemployment benefits sooner in some states (check your state's rules).
- Does it require you to waive all legal claims against the company? (Most do. Read this section of the agreement carefully.)
- Is there a non-disparagement clause? (Very common. This restricts what you can say publicly about the company. Make sure you understand the scope.)
- Does it include a non-compete or non-solicitation clause, and is it enforceable in your state? (Non-competes are unenforceable in California and several other states. Know your jurisdiction.)

### Negotiation Leverage Points

You have more leverage than you think if any of the following apply:
- You have been with the company for more than two years (you are owed more than a junior employee)
- You were recently passed over for promotion despite strong performance reviews (pattern of unfair treatment)
- Your layoff disproportionately affected a protected class and you are a member of it (potential discrimination claim, even if unintentional)
- You have unvested equity that is close to a vesting cliff

If any of these apply, consult an employment attorney before signing. A single consultation call can identify whether you have grounds to ask for a better package. Many attorneys work on contingency for employment cases.

### COBRA Healthcare Costs

COBRA allows you to continue your employer-sponsored health insurance for up to 18 months after termination, but you pay the full premium, including the employer's share, plus a 2% administrative fee. This is almost always expensive.

Before electing COBRA, compare to:
- Your spouse or partner's employer plan (if available)
- ACA marketplace plans at healthcare.gov (you qualify for a special enrollment period immediately after job loss)
- Medicaid, if your income will be low enough to qualify during the gap

Request the exact COBRA premium from your HR department before you decide. Know the number before your coverage lapses.

### Equity and Vesting

If you had stock options or RSUs, review your equity agreement immediately. Key questions:
- How long do you have to exercise vested options after termination? (Common windows are 90 days for ISOs, sometimes longer for NSOs)
- Does the layoff count as a qualifying termination that accelerates vesting? (Some agreements include this provision)
- What happens to unvested equity? (Almost always forfeited, but confirm)

If you have a significant amount of vested options close to expiration, this is worth discussing with a financial advisor. Exercising options has tax implications that vary based on option type.

## The Rapid Outreach Strategy

Speed of outreach directly correlates with speed of reemployment. The goal in the first two weeks is to activate your existing network before going to cold job applications. Most positions are filled through referrals, not job boards.

### LinkedIn Announcement Template

Post this within the first three to five days. Keep it professional, forward-looking, and brief.

---

"I was recently part of a layoff at [Company Name] and am now actively exploring my next opportunity.

I spent [X years] at [Company Name] working on [brief description of work: e.g., "backend infrastructure serving 5M daily users" or "product strategy for the enterprise segment"]. I am proud of what we built.

I am looking for roles in [2 to 3 specific areas, e.g., "engineering leadership, distributed systems, or platform engineering"] at companies where [brief description of what matters to you, e.g., "technical quality and team ownership are valued"].

If you know of an opening or just want to reconnect, I would welcome a conversation. You can reach me at [your personal email address].

Thank you to everyone who has already reached out."

---

**Do not write:**
- Anything negative about the company or the layoff decision
- Vague language like "open to opportunities" without specifics
- A long narrative about how hard this is

### Direct Email to Your Network

Send this individually to 20 to 30 people in your immediate professional network within the first week. Do not send a mass email. Personalize the first sentence.

---

Subject: Catching up + a heads up

Hi [Name],

I hope you are well. I wanted to reach out with a quick update: I was recently laid off from [Company] as part of a larger reduction in force and am starting to look for my next role.

I am targeting [role type or industry]. If you know of anything relevant or anyone I should talk to, I would genuinely appreciate an introduction or a name to contact.

Either way, I would love to reconnect when you have time. A 20-minute call would mean a lot.

[Your name]
[Phone number]
[LinkedIn URL]

---

Follow up once if you do not hear back in seven to ten days. Once only.

## Explaining the Gap

You will be asked about the layoff in every interview. Your answer must be prepared in advance. Unprepared answers tend to come out with unintended bitterness, excessive explanation, or self-deprecation, all of which hurt your candidacy.

### The Core Script

The answer should take roughly 20 to 30 seconds. No more.

"I was part of a layoff at [Company Name] in [month/year]. The company [brief factual explanation: reduced its engineering headcount by X%, or restructured the product team, or faced a funding contraction]. My role was eliminated along with several others. I used the time to be deliberate about my next step, and that is what brought me here."

Then redirect: "I am happy to answer any questions about it, but I would rather spend most of our time on what I can bring to this role."

### If They Ask for More Detail

"Was it a surprise?" Answer honestly: "It was, yes" or "There had been some signals" works fine. Do not speculate about whether it was fair.

"How did you feel about it?" This is an unusual question but it comes up. "It was disappointing. I was proud of the work I did there. But I understand that headcount decisions are driven by business constraints, and I do not have any bitterness about it. I am focused on what is next."

"Why you specifically?" "I genuinely do not know the full criteria. What I know is that the business made a structural decision and my role was included. My performance reviews were strong, and I left on good terms with my manager and colleagues, all of whom I am still in contact with."

**What never to say:**
- Anything that implies mismanagement by the company ("they had no idea what they were doing")
- Anything that implies you are still processing it emotionally ("it has been really hard")
- Anything that sounds like you are pleading your case for why you should not have been laid off
- Speculation about internal politics or who made the decision
