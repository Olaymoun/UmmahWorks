# Evaluating Product and Project Ethics

Not every project you are assigned will align with your values. Some will involve products you find troubling. Others will require you to build systems that could cause harm to users, communities, or society. Knowing how to evaluate these situations methodically and respond professionally is a fundamental career skill. This document gives you a repeatable process.

---

## Identifying the Conflict

Before taking any action, you need to correctly categorize the ethical concern. There is an important distinction between a hard ethical line and a personal preference. Conflating the two leads to either overreaction (escalating a preference as a moral crisis) or underreaction (dismissing a real harm as a personal bias).

### Hard Ethical Line vs. Personal Preference

A **hard ethical line** involves demonstrable harm to identifiable people or groups. Ask yourself the following questions:

- Will this product or system cause physical, financial, psychological, or social harm to users or third parties?
- Does it involve deception: concealing material facts from users, manipulating their behavior against their interests, or misrepresenting the product?
- Does it involve illegal activity, including violations of privacy law, labor law, discrimination law, or consumer protection law?
- Does it extract value from vulnerable populations (children, low-income users, people in crisis) in ways that exceed informed consent?
- Does it involve weapons systems, mass surveillance, or infrastructure used primarily for suppression or coercion?

If you can answer yes to any of these questions with specific, concrete evidence (not hypothetical concern), you are dealing with a hard ethical line.

A **personal preference** involves disagreement with a business model, industry, or product category that does not meet the threshold above. Examples:

- You dislike the gambling industry but the product itself is legal and compliant with regulations
- You disagree with the company's marketing strategy but the product is not harmful
- The company works in a sector you find personally distasteful (alcohol, meat production, certain financial products) but the work itself is legal and the users are not being deceived

### The Documentation Step

Before doing anything else, write down your concern in concrete terms. Use this template:

**The concern:** [State the specific feature, system, or project]
**The potential harm:** [Describe who is harmed and how, specifically]
**The evidence:** [What do you know for certain vs. what are you inferring?]
**The category:** [Hard ethical line / personal preference / unclear]

If you cannot fill in "The potential harm" with specific people and specific consequences, your concern may be a preference rather than an ethical violation. That does not make it unimportant, but it changes how you should respond.

## The Reassignment Request

If you have identified a hard ethical concern, or a preference-based discomfort strong enough that it would affect your ability to do quality work, the next step is requesting reassignment. This conversation is about professional judgment, not moral lecturing. Your goal is to move to a different project without burning your relationship with your manager or flagging yourself as difficult to work with.

### The Script

Request a private meeting. Do not raise this in a group setting or over chat.

Open by anchoring to your professional effectiveness, not your personal ethics:

"I wanted to talk to you about the [project name] work. I have been reflecting on my fit for this project, and I want to be honest with you: I am not sure I am the right person to do it well. I have some concerns about [specific aspect] that I think would affect the quality of my contribution. I do not think it is fair to the project for me to be less than fully committed to it."

If your manager asks you to elaborate, be direct but stay focused on the work outcome:

"I have some reservations about [specific feature or approach] and how it [specific concern]. I am not trying to make a policy argument. I just know myself well enough to know that this is going to be a distraction for me, and the project deserves someone who is all-in."

Then propose an alternative immediately:

"I would like to propose that [colleague name, or 'someone else on the team'] takes the lead on this, and I take on [alternative project or responsibility] instead. I am committed to a clean handoff and making sure whoever takes it over has everything they need."

### What to Avoid

- Do not moralize at your manager. You are not there to change their mind about the ethics of the project.
- Do not frame this as a threat or ultimatum in the first conversation.
- Do not discuss this with colleagues before talking to your manager.
- Do not document the conversation in writing unless the manager pushes back in a way that feels retaliatory.

### If the Request Is Denied

If your manager declines the reassignment and you are required to continue on the project, you have two options: comply while quietly accelerating your exit timeline (see the Building an Exit Timeline section below), or escalate to HR or a skip-level manager if the project involves a hard ethical line (not a preference). Escalation carries real risk. Do not escalate on preference-based concerns.

## Vetting Companies Pre-Interview

The most effective time to evaluate a company's ethics is before you apply, not after you have an offer in hand. Offer pressure is real. Candidates consistently accept roles they should not once compensation is on the table. Do the research first.

### Revenue Model Red Flags

Understand exactly how the company makes money before your first interview. Ask yourself: who pays, who uses, and are those the same person?

**Red flag: The user is the product, not the customer.**
If the company's primary revenue comes from advertising or data brokerage and its users do not pay directly, the incentive structure is misaligned. The company is optimizing for user engagement at all costs rather than user welfare.

**Red flag: Predatory pricing structures targeting vulnerable users.**
Payday loan platforms, high-interest buy-now-pay-later products targeting low-income users, and subscription services designed to be difficult to cancel all fall into this category.

**Red flag: Regulatory evasion as a growth strategy.**
If a company's growth narrative is explicitly built on operating in a legal gray area before regulators catch up, you are one enforcement action away from a layoff. This is both an ethical and a career risk.

**Red flag: Revenue from government surveillance contracts.**
Companies that build technology sold to law enforcement or government agencies for mass surveillance purposes. Research the customer list, not just the product description.

**Red flag: Dark patterns as core UX.**
Search for the company's product in academic research or journalism. Sites like the Dark Patterns Hall of Fame, investigative tech journalism, and FTC complaint databases surface a lot.

### Research Sources

- SEC filings (for public companies): 10-K and 10-Q reports describe revenue sources, risk factors, and legal proceedings in plain language
- FTC and state attorney general complaint databases
- Glassdoor reviews filtered by "ethics" and "culture" (look for patterns, not outliers)
- Crunchbase and Pitchbook for investor background (investors shape company direction)
- Press coverage from the past 18 months in outlets that cover the relevant industry

## Building an Exit Timeline

If your company pivots into a product area you cannot ethically support, and reassignment is not available, you need to leave. The exit should be quiet, professional, and on your terms. Abrupt departures driven by public drama help no one, including you.

### Step 1: Define Your Hard Deadline

Determine how long you can remain without compromising your integrity or professional reputation. For most people, this is three to six months. Write the date down and treat it as a non-negotiable constraint.

### Step 2: Assess Your Financial Position

Before you can leave, you need to know what you can afford. Calculate:
- Your current monthly fixed expenses (rent, debt payments, insurance, food)
- Your current liquid savings (checking, savings, accessible investment accounts)
- Your runway: savings divided by monthly expenses
- Your target runway: most advisors recommend a minimum of three to six months

If your runway is below your minimum threshold, your first priority is building it before your deadline. Reduce discretionary spending immediately.

### Step 3: Begin a Quiet Job Search

Do not announce the search internally. Do not use company equipment or networks. Do not complain to colleagues about the company pivot.

Begin by refreshing your resume, updating your LinkedIn (set job-seeking status to "open to work" visible only to recruiters, not your network), and reaching out directly to two or three people in your immediate professional network. Warm referrals move faster than cold applications.

### Step 4: Sequence Your Departure Responsibly

- Sign the offer at your new job before resigning from the current one.
- Prepare your resignation letter before the conversation (see the Resignation Protocol document).
- Plan your last day so you do not leave mid-project if at all possible. Leaving cleanly protects references.
- Do not explain the ethical pivot as the reason in your resignation unless you have a strong relationship with your manager and trust their discretion. The professional reason ("I found an opportunity that is a better fit for where I want to go") is sufficient.
