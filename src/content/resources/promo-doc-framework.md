# Promotion Document Framework

**Target Audience:** Mid-to-senior level engineers and product managers preparing their promotion cases.

---

## The Anatomy of a Promo Doc

A promotion document is not a resume. It is a structured argument. Its job is to prove, with evidence, that you are already operating at the next level, not that you have the potential to. Every strong promo doc shares the same four-part structure regardless of company or role.

### Part 1: Executive Summary (1 paragraph)

This is the top of your document. Write it last, after everything else is complete. Its job is to answer one question in 4 to 6 sentences: "Why should this person be promoted, and why now?"

The executive summary names your current level, your target level, and makes the case in summary form. It references the 2 or 3 most significant contributions from the body of the document. It is written for a senior leader who may not read past the first paragraph.

**What to include:**
- Your current title and target title
- The time period being evaluated
- 2 to 3 high-level contributions with quantified impact
- A one-sentence closing statement establishing readiness

### Part 2: Scope and Complexity

This section establishes the scale of the work you own. The goal is to make it undeniable that the problems you handle belong to the level you are targeting.

**What to include:**
- Number and type of systems, projects, or features you own or lead
- The size of the codebase, user base, or business surface you affect
- The cross-functional footprint of your work (how many teams, stakeholders, or partners your work touches)
- The ambiguity level: were you handed a spec, or did you define the problem yourself?

**Why this matters:** Promotion committees do not just evaluate output. They evaluate whether the scope of your work matches the target level's expectations. An engineer solving well-defined problems with clear requirements is operating at one level; an engineer defining the problem, designing the system, and aligning stakeholders across three teams is operating at another.

### Part 3: Business Impact

This is the most important section. Every item here should answer: "So what?" Listing that you built a feature is not impact. Stating that the feature reduced support tickets by 38% in the first quarter is impact.

**What to include:**
- Outcomes tied to business metrics (revenue, cost, reliability, user growth, retention)
- Quantified before/after comparisons where possible
- The scope of who benefited (how many users, which teams, what percentage of the product)
- Any risk mitigation or crisis response you owned, with the stakes clearly stated

If you do not have exact numbers, use estimates with clear sourcing. "Approximately 20% reduction based on oncall ticket volume" is acceptable. Vague qualitative statements like "significantly improved performance" are not.

### Part 4: Leadership and Mentorship

Senior individual contributors and managers alike are expected to multiply the output of those around them. This section documents how your presence makes other people better and the team more effective.

**What to include:**
- Engineers or PMs you have formally or informally mentored, and what changed as a result
- Technical or process improvements you introduced that others adopted
- Onboarding, documentation, or tooling contributions that reduced friction for the team
- Examples of you driving alignment in ambiguous or contentious situations
- Times you unblocked others or absorbed complexity so junior team members could move faster

## Quantifying Impact vs. Listing Tasks

The most common mistake in promo docs is describing what you did rather than what resulted from it. The table below shows the difference.

| Weak Phrasing (Task Listing) | Strong Phrasing (Business Impact) |
|---|---|
| "Refactored the authentication service." | "Refactored the authentication service, reducing average login latency from 800ms to 120ms, directly improving conversion on the onboarding flow by 6%." |
| "Led the Q3 payments migration." | "Led the Q3 payments migration to Stripe, enabling the company to launch in 3 new markets and generating an estimated $2.1M in incremental ARR." |
| "Mentored two junior engineers." | "Mentored two junior engineers who each shipped their first independently owned projects within 90 days, reducing lead time on the team's backlog by an estimated 25%." |
| "Built the internal analytics dashboard." | "Built the internal analytics dashboard used by the data, product, and finance teams daily, replacing a manual reporting process that previously consumed 6 hours per week of analyst time." |
| "Reduced tech debt in the API layer." | "Reduced tech debt in the API layer, cutting p99 latency from 4.2s to 0.9s and eliminating a category of timeout errors that had generated an average of 12 oncall pages per month." |
| "Collaborated with design and product." | "Drove alignment between design, product, and engineering on the checkout redesign, resolving a 3-week stakeholder impasse that had blocked the Q2 launch." |
| "Improved test coverage." | "Increased test coverage from 34% to 81% on the core billing module, resulting in zero production incidents from that surface in the two quarters following the change." |

The pattern is consistent: state the action, then state the measured result. If a measured result is not available, state the mechanism by which impact was created (who used it, how often, what decision it informed).

## The Annotated Template

Use this as your base document. Replace every bracketed instruction with your own content.

---

**[YOUR NAME] | Promotion Case: [CURRENT LEVEL] to [TARGET LEVEL]**
**Review Period: [START DATE] to [END DATE]**
**Submitted: [DATE]**
**Manager: [MANAGER NAME]**

---

### Executive Summary

[Write this section last. In 4 to 6 sentences, state your current role, the level you are targeting, and the top 2 to 3 reasons the promotion is warranted now. Reference specific projects from the sections below. Close with one sentence that anchors your readiness: e.g., "Across this review period, I have consistently operated at the [TARGET LEVEL] scope and am ready to formalize that transition."]

---

### Scope and Complexity

**Systems and Projects Owned:**
[List the 3 to 5 most significant projects or systems you owned or led during this period. For each, include: the project name, your role (owner, lead, contributor), and 1 to 2 sentences on the scope and complexity involved.]

**Cross-Functional Reach:**
[Describe the teams, stakeholders, or external partners your work affected. Quantify where possible: "Coordinated with 4 product teams across 2 business units" is more compelling than "worked cross-functionally."]

**Ambiguity and Autonomy:**
[Describe the highest-ambiguity problem you navigated in this period. What was undefined when you started? What decisions did you make without a playbook? This establishes senior-level judgment.]

---

### Business Impact

**[Project or Initiative Name 1]**
- What you did: [1 sentence]
- Outcome: [1 to 2 sentences with metrics. E.g., "Reduced [X] by [Y]%, resulting in [Z business result]."]

**[Project or Initiative Name 2]**
- What you did: [1 sentence]
- Outcome: [1 to 2 sentences with metrics]

**[Project or Initiative Name 3]**
- What you did: [1 sentence]
- Outcome: [1 to 2 sentences with metrics]

[Add as many projects as are relevant. 3 to 5 is typically the right range. Quality over quantity: three strong examples with hard metrics beat seven vague ones.]

---

### Leadership and Mentorship

**Mentorship:**
[Name 1 to 3 people you mentored or supported directly. For each: describe what you did (reviewed code, pair-programmed, ran 1:1s, helped them scope a project) and what changed as a result (they shipped independently, they got positive feedback in their own review, they onboarded faster).]

**Team Multiplier Work:**
[Describe any process changes, tooling improvements, or documentation you created that made the broader team more effective. Be specific: "Wrote the incident response runbook now used by all oncall engineers" is concrete. "Improved team processes" is not.]

**Alignment and Influence:**
[Describe 1 to 2 situations where you drove alignment in a situation that was ambiguous, contested, or cross-functional. What was the disagreement or ambiguity? How did you resolve it? What was the result?]

## The Review Process

Starting the document too late is the most common avoidable mistake. The typical promotion cycle at most companies includes a self-review period, peer feedback collection, manager calibration, and a committee review. You need to be ahead of each step.

### Recommended Timeline

**8 to 10 weeks before cycle close:**
- Begin drafting. Start with the Business Impact section using your strongest 3 projects. Do not wait for perfect metrics.
- Pull data: look at dashboards, tickets closed, pages resolved, feature adoption numbers, user feedback. Take notes now before the details fade.

**6 to 7 weeks before cycle close:**
- Complete the first full draft of all four sections.
- Send the draft to one trusted peer (ideally someone who has been promoted recently or is already at the target level). Ask specifically: "Does the impact language sound concrete and measurable, or vague?"

**4 to 5 weeks before cycle close:**
- Share the draft with your manager. Frame it as: "I want to make sure my narrative aligns with how you plan to represent my case in calibration."
- Incorporate feedback. If your manager suggests adding or removing examples, take that seriously. They know what the committee responds to.

**2 to 3 weeks before cycle close:**
- Final revisions. Confirm all metrics are accurate. Double-check that every major project mentioned is one your manager can speak to in the room.
- Ask your manager directly: "Is there anything missing from this document that would make my case harder to defend?"

**1 week before cycle close:**
- Submit. Do not wait for the last day. Late submissions are sometimes excluded from the current cycle.

### Who Should Review Your Document

1. **Your manager (required).** They will represent you in the calibration room. They need to be fluent in your narrative.
2. **A trusted peer at or above the target level (strongly recommended).** They can give you signal on whether your framing is calibrated correctly for the level.
3. **Someone who has been through the process recently at the same company (optional but valuable).** Process details vary by org. Internal knowledge is hard to replicate.

Do not share your document with a large group. Keep reviewers to 2 or 3. More feedback introduces noise and often results in a diluted document.
