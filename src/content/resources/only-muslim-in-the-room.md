# On being the only Muslim in the room, and staying anyway.

*By Dr. Ayesha Rahman, Principal Engineer*

---

I spent my first five years at a big company feeling like a guest who had overstayed her welcome.

When you are the only Muslim in the room, your daily existence requires a layer of mental calculus that your peers do not share. I remember the specific anxiety of my early career clearly. I was a junior software engineer, desperate to prove I belonged in a highly competitive environment. I wanted my code to be flawless, my system designs to be bulletproof, and my presence to be entirely frictionless.

But my presence was not frictionless. My faith requires space, time, and routine, and corporate America is not inherently built to accommodate those things.

In those early days, the friction was literal. We worked in a sprawling, state-of-the-art campus, but there were prayer spaces that did not exist. Finding a quiet place to pray Dhuhr or Asr became a daily, stressful reconnaissance mission. I knew which stairwells on the third floor were rarely used. I knew which unused conference rooms had blinds that actually closed. I would sneak away with my travel mat, heart pounding, terrified that a senior manager would open the door and find me prostrating on the carpet. I felt absurd, but I also felt trapped.

Then came the scheduling conflicts. The tech industry moves fast, and calendars are treated as communal property. Fridays were a recurring source of dread. Time and time again, I found myself trapped in critical syncs and all-hands meetings that ran through Jummah. I would sit in those conference rooms, watching the clock tick past the time I needed to leave, paralyzed by the fear that walking out would signal I was not a team player.

I was exhausted. I was carrying the weight of my workload alongside the hidden burden of shrinking myself to fit into a mold that was never designed for me.

---

By my third year, I had drafted my resignation letter. I was entirely convinced that I could not be a devout Muslim and a successful engineer in this environment. I thought about leaving tech altogether. I know many of you reading this are engineers wondering whether you should leave, too. You are tired of being the odd one out at team happy hours. You are tired of explaining Ramadan every single year. You are tired of feeling like your spiritual obligations are an inconvenience to the product roadmap.

But I did not submit that resignation letter. Instead, I decided that if I was going to leave, I would at least try doing things on my terms first. I had nothing to lose.

I stopped sneaking around. I stopped asking for permission to practice my faith. I started establishing the firm boundaries that made the work sustainable.

---

The shift in my approach was subtle but profound. I put a hard, unyielding block on my calendar for Friday afternoons. When a project manager scheduled a launch meeting over it, I did not apologize or ask if we could move it. I simply declined the invitation and left a polite note:

> "I am unavailable at this time due to a weekly religious commitment. I look forward to catching up on the recording, or we can reschedule for 3:00 PM."

I expected pushback. I expected anger. What I received, to my absolute shock, was a simple: *No problem, we will move it to 3:00 PM.*

I took that momentum to the facilities team. I stopped looking for empty stairwells. I wrote a formal, professional email outlining the need for a dedicated wellness and prayer space in our building, citing company diversity initiatives. Within a month, a small, quiet room was designated for that exact purpose.

What I learned in those pivotal years is that corporate systems are entirely indifferent to you. They are not maliciously trying to exclude you; they simply do not know you exist until you force them to acknowledge you. People will take as much of your time and energy as you allow them to. The moment you define your boundaries clearly, professionally, and without guilt, the system will adjust around you.

---

I stayed anyway. I am now a Principal Engineer. I lead massive projects, I sit in the rooms where technical strategy is decided, and I do not compromise my faith to do it.

I stayed because walking away means the next Muslim graduate hired by this company will have to fight the exact same battles I did. I stayed because our presence normalizes our existence. When you pray openly, when you block your calendar for Jummah, when you request halal food for the team offsite, you are doing the quiet, necessary work of structural change. You are paving the road for the junior engineer who will arrive five years from now, who will walk into a building with a prayer room and a team that already knows not to schedule meetings on Friday afternoons.

> Do not leave because the room is not built for you. Stay, set your boundaries, and force the room to expand.
