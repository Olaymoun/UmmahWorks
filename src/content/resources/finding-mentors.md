# Building a Personal Board of Directors

**Target Audience:** Professionals at any level who want to build a genuine advisory network rather than relying on formal mentorship programs that rarely deliver what they promise.

---

## Moving Beyond "Will You Be My Mentor"

The formal mentorship request fails almost every time it is used. A cold email or LinkedIn message asking someone to be your mentor puts the recipient in an uncomfortable position: saying yes commits them to an undefined, open-ended time obligation; saying no feels unkind. Most people respond by saying nothing at all.

The underlying problem is that mentorship relationships are built through repeated interaction, not initiated through a single request. The people who ended up as your best professional advisors almost certainly did not receive a formal mentorship request from you. They gave you feedback on something, answered a question well, or collaborated with you on work, and the relationship deepened over time.

The approach that consistently works is contribution before request. Build value in the relationship before you make any ask.

**How to do this in practice:**

Find someone whose work or thinking you genuinely respect. Read what they have written. Watch their talks. Understand their perspective before reaching out. Then engage with their work specifically: send a thoughtful response to something they published, ask a precise question about a decision they described publicly, or share something you built that was directly influenced by their thinking.

This is genuine engagement with someone's ideas, which is the actual foundation of mentorship relationships. The practical difference is that instead of asking "will you be my mentor," you are asking "I had a specific question about the decision you described in your talk. Can I ask you about it?" That question is easy to answer. It requires ten minutes. It creates the first real interaction.

## The Four Roles You Need

Think of your professional support network as four distinct roles, each serving a different purpose. Most people rely on one or two and leave the others empty, which leaves real gaps in what their network can actually do for them.

**The Sponsor** is someone who advocates for you in rooms you are not in. Sponsors are typically senior leaders who have seen your work directly and will recommend you for opportunities, projects, or roles. You cannot ask for a sponsor directly. Sponsorship is earned by producing work that makes a sponsor's recommendation credible. Your job is to make sure your best work is visible to people senior enough to advocate for it.

**The Technical Guide** is a peer or near-peer with deep expertise in a specific area you are actively building toward. Not someone years ahead of you, but someone who has recently done what you are trying to do. Their advice is current and specific. They know the tools, the shortcuts, and the failure modes that are not in any documentation. These relationships often form laterally, through communities, open source projects, or shared conference experiences.

**The Career Strategist** is someone who has navigated a career arc similar to the one you want. They can help you think through long-term decisions: whether to take the management path, how to evaluate a particular company or opportunity, when to make a lateral move versus waiting for a promotion. This person does not need to know you deeply. They need to have the perspective you are currently missing.

**The Peer Accountability Partner** is a colleague at a similar level who you meet with regularly to share goals, report on progress, and hold each other to commitments. This is the least glamorous of the four roles and the most consistently useful. A monthly 30-minute call with one trusted peer where you each say "here is what I said I would do, here is what I actually did, and here is what I am committing to next" is worth more than most formal mentorship programs that exist at companies.

## Preparing for Mentorship Meetings

Nothing kills a mentorship relationship faster than a meeting where you show up with no agenda, say "I just wanted to pick your brain," and proceed to ask questions that could have been answered with basic research.

The person you are meeting with has given you their most limited resource: their time. Your job is to demonstrate that you are worth it and that they can leave the meeting having contributed something genuinely useful.

Use this agenda template for every mentorship conversation. Send it at least 48 hours before the meeting.

---

**Pre-read:**

**Context:** [Two to three sentences on your current role and where you are in your career. They may not remember the details from your last conversation.]

**What I am working on:** [The most relevant thing you are currently building or navigating. One short paragraph.]

**What I have already tried:** [What you have already attempted before bringing it to this conversation. This signals that you do not come before doing your own thinking first.]

**My specific question:** [One to two precise questions you want to answer in this meeting. Not "what do you think about my career" but "I am deciding between taking this management role now or staying IC for another 18 months. Here is what I know and here is what I cannot figure out. What would you weight differently?"]

---

Sending this document in advance has two effects. It forces you to get specific, which improves the quality of your own thinking before the conversation even starts. And it gives the mentor time to think before the meeting, which produces substantially better advice.

## Following Up on Advice

The single highest-leverage thing you can do to maintain a mentorship relationship long-term is to follow up with a specific update on how you applied the advice you received.

Send this email 30 days after any substantive mentorship conversation:

---

**Subject:** Update on the advice you gave me about [topic]

Hi [Name],

I wanted to follow up on the conversation we had [date] about [specific topic].

You suggested [specific advice]. Here is what I did: [what you actually tried, in concrete terms].

Here is what happened: [the outcome, good or bad, with specifics].

[If it worked:] It worked better than I expected because [reason]. Thank you for the push in that direction.

[If it did not work as planned:] It did not go as expected because [reason]. I learned [specific lesson] from the attempt and I am adjusting my approach.

I have one follow-up question if you have a moment: [one precise question that emerged from the experience].

Thank you again for your time.

[Your name]

---

This message is rare. Most people who receive mentoring advice never report back on what happened. When you do, you accomplish three things: you demonstrate that you took the advice seriously, you give the mentor a visible result (which is intrinsically satisfying), and you give yourself a natural reason to keep the conversation going. The mentor who sees their advice produce a real outcome is the mentor who takes your next call.
