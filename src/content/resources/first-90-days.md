# The First 90 Days Onboarding Framework

**Target Audience:** Professionals starting a new job who want to build credibility quickly, understand the organization, and establish the right working norms from the start.

---

## The Listening Tour

The most common mistake new employees make is trying to add value before they understand the system they have joined. Offering solutions before you understand the problems is almost always counterproductive. It signals low situational awareness and often proposes changes that have already been tried and failed for reasons you do not know yet.

The listening tour is a structured series of conversations in your first 30 days. It replaces the instinct to demonstrate competence with the habit of demonstrating curiosity, which is far more rare and far more respected in most organizations.

**Who to meet in your first 30 days:**

For every new role, identify and schedule 20-minute conversations with the following people:

- Your direct manager (multiple times, already happening in 1:1s)
- Every person on your immediate team
- Two or three people on adjacent teams you will work with regularly
- One person each in the following functions: product, design, data or analytics, and a customer-facing role (sales, support, or success)
- One person who has been at the company for more than three years (institutional memory)
- One person who joined within the last six months (recent experience navigating your same onboarding)

**The four standard questions to ask every person:**

1. "What is working well on this team or in this organization that I should be careful not to disrupt?"
2. "What is the most frustrating part of working here that no one seems to be addressing?"
3. "Who should I be talking to who is not already on my list?"
4. "Is there anything you wish someone had told you when you started here?"

Take notes during every conversation. After all your conversations are complete, look for patterns. The frustrations that come up in five separate conversations are the real problems. The things that get consistent praise tell you what the culture actually values. This synthesis is more useful than any document handed to you during onboarding.

## Securing an Early Win

By day 45 to 60, you should be looking for one small, visible problem to solve. Not your largest mandate. Not the project your manager has already outlined for the next six months. A small, real problem that you can fix quickly and that other people will notice.

The criteria for a good early win:

**Low risk if you fail.** Pick something where a mistake will not cause a serious incident or block other people's work.

**High visibility to at least two or three people.** The fix should benefit someone other than yourself. A documented process, an automation, a bug fix that was annoying multiple people, a missing piece of documentation.

**Completable in two to three weeks.** An early win that takes six months is not an early win.

**Within your existing competence.** This is not the time to stretch aggressively. Execute something clean and reliable.

A few examples of good early wins: fixing a broken test suite that the team was manually working around; writing the runbook that does not exist for a common onboarding task; building a small dashboard the team wanted but had not prioritized; cleaning up a documentation page that was confusing new contributors.

When you deliver the win, send a brief written update to your manager and, if appropriate, the people it directly benefited. This is not self-promotion. It is closing the loop. The written update also becomes the first entry in your brag document.

## Documenting the Undocumented

Every organization has institutional knowledge that exists only in people's heads. Tribal knowledge about why a system works a particular way, which vendor cannot be trusted on deadlines, how the budget process actually works versus how it is supposed to work.

As a new employee, you are in a unique position: you encounter this knowledge fresh, you notice the gaps, and you remember the confusion. In six months you will have normalized it. Right now, you can capture it.

Keep a confusion log during your onboarding. Every time you encounter something confusing, missing, or unclear, write it down with enough detail that someone else could understand the gap. As you learn the answers, write those down too.

By the end of your first 90 days, convert this log into actual documentation. This is your most direct contribution to the team as a new employee. You are not disrupting anything. You are making it easier for the next person who joins after you.

Share the documentation with your manager with a simple note:

> "I kept a log of things that confused me during onboarding and wrote up the answers as I learned them. I thought this might be useful for the next person who joins. Let me know if there is a better place to put this or if any of it needs correction."

This approach demonstrates initiative, attention to detail, and genuine investment in the team without overstepping or proposing large changes before you understand the context.

## Establishing Boundaries Early

The most important time to establish your working norms is the first two weeks, not month three. Whatever you agree to in your first 30 days becomes the baseline assumption for your entire tenure at that company. Patterns established early are very hard to change later without friction.

**Working hours and availability.** Be explicit about when you are reachable. "I am generally available from 9 AM to 6 PM and check messages outside those hours if something is marked urgent" is a complete and professional statement. You do not need permission to state this. Set it in your Slack status, your email signature, and mention it in your onboarding conversations with your manager.

**Response time expectations.** If you do not want to be expected to respond to Slack messages within five minutes on a Sunday, do not respond to Slack messages within five minutes on a Sunday. Patterns established in the first month become norms. Do not establish patterns you are not prepared to sustain indefinitely.

**Religious accommodations.** Weeks one and two are the correct time to set up your calendar blocks, inform your manager, and submit any formal accommodation requests. The Workplace Accommodations guide covers the exact language and process for this. Do it early so it is never unusual. By month two, it is simply how your calendar works.

**Deep work time.** Block two to three two-hour windows on your calendar each week for focused work. Mark them busy. Do this before your calendar fills with standing meetings. These blocks are significantly harder to establish once a meeting-heavy culture has claimed your time.
