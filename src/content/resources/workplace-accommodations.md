# Navigating Workplace Accommodations

**Target Audience:** Professionals needing to establish religious, medical, or personal schedule boundaries in corporate environments.

---

## The Calendar Block

The most effective way to protect recurring time is to block it on your calendar before anyone else claims it. You do not need permission to block time on your own calendar. Treat it the same way you would treat a standing team sync or a recurring 1:1 with your manager. The block communicates the time is unavailable. The reason is secondary.

### Google Calendar: Step-by-Step

1. Open Google Calendar and navigate to the recurring time slot you need (e.g., Friday 12:30 PM to 2:00 PM).
2. Click the time slot and select **More options** to open the full event editor.
3. Set the title. Use something neutral and professional: **"Focus Block"**, **"Midday Break"**, or **"Standing Commitment"**. You are not required to write "Jumu'ah" or "Prayer" unless you choose to.
4. Set the event to **Repeat weekly** (or the correct recurrence for your schedule).
5. Under **Status**, set it to **"Busy"**. This is critical. If it is set to "Free," meeting scheduling tools will override the block.
6. Under **Visibility**, set it to **"Private"**. This means guests and coworkers who view your calendar will see "Busy (Private)" rather than the event title.
7. Save the event.

For daily prayer blocks (e.g., Dhuhr or Asr), create 15 to 20 minute blocks and apply the same settings. You can stack two or three across the day without them looking unusual.

### Outlook: Step-by-Step

1. Open Outlook Calendar and click **New Event** or click the target time slot.
2. Title the event using the same neutral language above.
3. Set the recurrence under **Recurrence** or **Repeat**.
4. Under **Show As**, select **"Busy"** (not "Free" or "Tentative").
5. Under **Private**, check the **Private** checkbox (lock icon). This hides the event details from anyone viewing your calendar.
6. Save and close.

### Handling Automatic Scheduling Tools

Many companies use tools like Calendly, Clockwise, or Google's "Meet with" feature that auto-schedule on your behalf. These tools respect "Busy" blocks and will not place meetings over them. If you are using Clockwise's "Focus Time" feature, make sure your manually created blocks are marked Busy; Clockwise treats Free blocks as eligible for scheduling.

If a tool schedules over your block anyway, you have standing to decline the meeting because your calendar showed unavailable. The Exact Scripts section below covers the exact language for this.

## The HR/Manager Conversation

You have two paths: proactive disclosure or reactive disclosure. Proactive disclosure means you inform your manager before any conflict occurs. Reactive disclosure means you address it after a scheduling conflict arises. Proactive is almost always cleaner.

### When to Have the Conversation

The best time is during your first or second week on the job, ideally in the context of a 1:1 where you are already discussing working style and schedule preferences. This normalizes it as a logistics conversation rather than a request or an exception.

### What to Say (The Framework)

Keep the conversation brief and concrete. You are not asking for a favor. You are informing your manager of a fixed constraint in your schedule, the same way you would tell them you have a standing school pickup at 5:00 PM.

**The four elements:**

1. **The fact:** State the commitment clearly and without apology.
2. **The scope:** Define when it applies and how often.
3. **The coverage plan:** Briefly mention how you handle anything urgent that falls in that window.
4. **The invitation to ask questions:** Keep the door open without making it feel like a negotiation.

**Spoken example:**

> "I wanted to flag early on that I have a standing commitment on Friday afternoons from about 12:30 to 1:30. I'll have my calendar blocked. If anything urgent comes up in that window I'm happy to sync right after, and I always check messages before I step away. Is there anything recurring on Fridays I should plan around?"

That is the entire conversation. You do not need to explain what the commitment is unless you choose to. Most managers will accept this without question.

### If Your Manager Pushes Back

If your manager asks for more detail or expresses concern, respond with specifics about coverage rather than justification of the practice. For example:

> "The block is consistent each week so I can plan around it. If there's a critical meeting in that window I'd want to know in advance so I can adjust or ensure someone else can represent the team."

This keeps the conversation on logistics. If the pushback continues and the accommodation is religious in nature, you have legal protections in most jurisdictions (covered briefly in the Establishing Dedicated Spaces section below).

## Exact Scripts

### Script 1: Setting Expectations During Onboarding (Email)

Use this in the first week of a new role. Send it to your direct manager after an initial verbal conversation, or instead of one if the relationship is still being established.

---

**Subject:** Quick Note on My Weekly Schedule

Hi [Manager Name],

As I'm getting settled in, I wanted to flag one recurring item on my schedule so it doesn't catch anyone off guard.

I have a standing commitment on Fridays from 12:30 PM to 1:30 PM. I've blocked that time on my calendar and set it to private/busy. Outside of that window I'm fully available, and I keep Slack open before and after in case anything time-sensitive comes up.

If there are any standing Friday meetings I should know about or plan around, let me know and I'll make sure to adjust accordingly.

Thanks,
[Your Name]

---

### Script 2: Declining a Meeting Scheduled Over a Blocked Time (Email or Slack)

Use this when a meeting invitation lands on a time you have already blocked. This is a decline, not a negotiation.

---

**Option A (Email decline):**

Hi [Organizer Name],

Thanks for the invite. I have a standing commitment at that time and won't be able to join. If my input is needed, I'm happy to review any materials async beforehand or sync after the meeting.

If this is a recurring series, a slightly earlier or later slot would work well for me. Let me know if that's an option.

Thanks,
[Your Name]

---

**Option B (Slack, more casual tone):**

Hey [Name], I have that slot blocked on Fridays. Can we shift it earlier, or is there an async way I can contribute? Happy to review notes after or weigh in beforehand.

---

Both versions decline clearly, offer an alternative, and do not require you to explain the reason for the block.

## Establishing Dedicated Spaces

If your workplace does not have a designated wellness or prayer room, you can request one through Facilities or HR. This is a procedural request and is often granted quickly because it requires minimal resources (usually just a room designation and a calendar booking link).

### Step-by-Step Request Process

**Step 1: Identify the right contact.** In most companies this is either Facilities, Office Operations, or HR. Check your internal directory or ask IT/admin who manages conference room bookings.

**Step 2: Frame it as a wellness room request.** This framing is broadly understood and inclusive. A "wellness room" is used for prayer, nursing, meditation, and mental health breaks. Requesting one as a shared resource rather than a personal one broadens its utility and makes it easier to approve.

**Step 3: Send a short written request.** Email is preferred because it creates a record.

**Sample request:**

> Hi [Facilities/HR Contact],
>
> I wanted to raise a request for a designated wellness or quiet room in the office. I'd use it briefly on a recurring basis during the workday, and I imagine others would benefit from having a private, quiet space available as well.
>
> If there's a room that could be designated for this or a booking process already in place, I'd appreciate being pointed in the right direction.
>
> Thanks, [Your Name]

**Step 4: Follow up once if no response in 5 business days.** A single follow-up is appropriate. If there is still no response, escalate to HR and reference the original message.

**Step 5: Understand your legal standing.** In the United States, Title VII of the Civil Rights Act requires employers to provide reasonable religious accommodations unless doing so creates undue hardship. Similar protections exist in the UK (Equality Act 2010), Canada, and the EU. "Undue hardship" for something like designating a quiet room is an extremely high bar. You do not need to cite legal code in your initial request, but knowing it exists strengthens your position if you face resistance.

**What to do if the request is denied:** Ask for the denial in writing and the reason for it. Then schedule a conversation with HR to discuss accommodation options. Document every step. In most cases a denial will not reach this stage, but having documentation protects you if it does.
