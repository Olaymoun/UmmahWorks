# Compensation Negotiation Framework

**Target Audience:** Professionals at the offer stage evaluating compensation packages.

---

## Handling the Initial Number

### The Psychology of the Pause

When a recruiter gives you the initial offer number on a call, your instinct may be to respond immediately. Resist that instinct completely. The pause is your most powerful tool in the first call, and most people never use it.

Here is the mechanism: recruiters expect negotiation. They have been trained for it. What they do not expect is measured silence followed by a calm request for time. That response signals two things simultaneously: (1) you are not desperate, and (2) you are taking the offer seriously enough to evaluate it properly. Both signal strengthen your position before you have said a single number.

**The Rule:** Never accept, counter, or reject an offer on the first call. Your only goal on that call is to gather information and buy time.

### What to Do on the First Call

**Step 1: Listen to the full offer.** Do not interrupt. Let the recruiter finish every component: base salary, equity (type, amount, vesting schedule), sign-on bonus, benefits, and start date. Take notes.

**Step 2: Express genuine interest.** This is not sycophancy. It is useful signaling. You want the recruiter to know you are a real candidate, not someone shopping offers.

**Step 3: Ask clarifying questions.** These serve two purposes: they show engagement, and they give you information you will need when you counter.

Good questions to ask on the first call:
- "Can you walk me through the equity component in more detail? Is it RSUs or options, and what's the vesting schedule?"
- "Is the sign-on bonus paid upfront or in installments?"
- "When does this offer expire, and is there flexibility on the start date?"
- "What's the review cycle for compensation adjustments?"

**Step 4: Use the pause and close.** After the recruiter has answered your questions, say something like this:

> "Thank you, I really appreciate the detail. This sounds like a strong offer and I'm genuinely excited about the role. I'd like to take a day or two to review everything carefully before I respond. Can I get back to you by [specific date, 2 to 3 business days out]?"

This closes the call cleanly. You have committed to a follow-up date, which shows professionalism. You have not accepted or countered. You have bought yourself time to prepare a proper response.

**What NOT to say on the first call:**
- "Wow, that sounds great!" (signals you may accept without negotiating)
- "That's a bit lower than I was hoping for" (tips your hand too early without leverage)
- "Let me think about it" without giving a specific callback date (leaves it open-ended, reduces urgency)

### After the Call

Immediately write down every component of the offer in full. Then research comparables using market data before you respond. Relevant sources include Levels.fyi (for tech roles), Glassdoor, LinkedIn Salary, and Payscale. Your goal is to understand where the offer sits relative to the market before you decide how hard to push.

## Evaluating the Package

Total compensation is not just the base salary number. A common mistake is to anchor on base salary while ignoring components that can represent 30 to 60 percent of total compensation at mid-to-senior levels.

### How to Calculate Total Compensation (TC)

**Base Salary:** The guaranteed annual cash component. This is the baseline.

**Equity (RSUs or Options):**
- For RSUs: Take the total grant value and divide it by the vesting period (typically 4 years). This gives you the annual equity value at the current share price.
- Example: $400,000 RSU grant over 4 years = $100,000/year in equity at current price.
- Note: RSU value fluctuates with the stock price. Do not count pre-IPO equity as reliable income.

**Sign-on Bonus:**
- Amortize a sign-on bonus over the tenure you expect to stay. If you receive a $50,000 sign-on and expect to stay 2 years, that is effectively $25,000/year.
- Watch for clawback provisions. Many sign-on bonuses must be repaid if you leave within 12 to 24 months. Read the terms before counting this as guaranteed money.

**Annual Bonus (if applicable):**
- If the offer includes a target annual bonus (e.g., 15% of base), calculate it as: Base x Bonus Percentage = Expected bonus. Treat this as variable, not guaranteed, unless you have a contractual floor.

**Benefits:**
- 401k match, health insurance, PTO, and remote work stipends have real monetary value. A company with strong 401k matching (e.g., 6% match with no cap) may be worth $5,000 to $10,000+ more per year than a company with no match.

### A Simple Comparison Table

| Component | Company A | Company B |
|---|---|---|
| Base Salary | $150,000 | $160,000 |
| Annual Equity (annualized) | $75,000 | $40,000 |
| Sign-on (amortized over 2 yr) | $12,500 | $0 |
| Target Bonus | $15,000 | $24,000 |
| 401k Match | $9,000 | $3,200 |
| **Total Estimated TC** | **$261,500** | **$227,200** |

In this example, Company A has a lower base salary but higher total compensation. Evaluating only the headline number would lead to the wrong decision.

## The Counter-Offer Scripts

You have done your research. Now you respond. All three templates below follow the same structural principle: acknowledge the offer positively, state your request specifically, anchor it to data or circumstances rather than personal need, and close with continued enthusiasm for the role.

**Never make the counter about your bills, your previous salary, or what you "need."** Frame everything around market data, your value, or bridging a verifiable gap.

---

### Template A: Asking for a Higher Base Salary Based on Market Data

**Subject:** Re: [Job Title] Offer from [Company Name]

Hi [Recruiter Name],

Thank you for the offer and for taking the time to walk me through the details. I'm genuinely excited about the role and the team, and I want to make this work.

After reviewing the full package, I'd like to discuss the base salary. Based on my research of comparable roles at this level in [market/location] (using Levels.fyi, LinkedIn Salary, and a few conversations with peers), the market range for this scope and experience is approximately [$X to $Y]. Given my background in [specific area of expertise] and the scope of the responsibilities outlined, I was hoping we could explore a base closer to [$specific target number].

I'm committed to this opportunity and want to move forward. Is there room to adjust the base in that direction?

Thanks again,
[Your Name]

---

**Notes on Template A:**
- Replace the target number with a specific figure, not a range. Giving a range lets the recruiter anchor to the low end.
- The target number should be 10 to 20 percent above the offer, depending on how low the offer is relative to market. Going higher than 20 percent risks appearing uninformed unless your research strongly supports it.
- Keep the tone collaborative, not adversarial.

---

### Template B: Asking for a Higher Sign-on Bonus to Bridge a Gap

**Subject:** Re: [Job Title] Offer from [Company Name]

Hi [Recruiter Name],

Thank you for putting together this offer. The base salary and equity are competitive, and I'm very interested in moving forward.

One thing I wanted to raise: I'm currently in the middle of a vesting cycle at my current company and will be leaving [approximate value] of unvested equity on the table by making this transition. I understand that's not always something a company can fully offset, but I wanted to ask whether there's any flexibility to increase the sign-on bonus to help bridge that gap.

If we could bring the sign-on to [$target amount], that would make the transition significantly easier for me and would allow me to commit to a start date quickly.

I'm very enthusiastic about this role and want to move forward. Is that something we can explore?

Thanks,
[Your Name]

---

**Notes on Template B:**
- This template is specifically for situations where you are leaving unvested equity or a pending bonus at your current employer.
- The sign-on counter is often easier to approve than a base salary change because it is a one-time cost rather than a recurring budget line.
- Be prepared to provide documentation if asked (a screenshot of your vesting schedule, for example).

---

### Template C: Leveraging a Competing Offer

**Subject:** Re: [Job Title] Offer from [Company Name]

Hi [Recruiter Name],

Thank you for the offer on the [Job Title] role. I want to be transparent with you because I genuinely want this opportunity to work out.

I currently have a competing offer from another company at [$X base / $Y total compensation]. That offer is for a comparable role and I have until [date] to respond. [Company Name] is my preferred choice based on [specific reason: the team, the product direction, the mission, etc.], and I would like to make this work without walking away from a higher offer.

Is there any flexibility to bring the compensation closer to [$specific target], or to explore other components of the package? I want to be straightforward rather than use this as a pressure tactic. My goal is to find a number that works for both sides.

Thank you for considering it,
[Your Name]

---

**Notes on Template C:**
- Only use this template if you have an actual competing offer. Using a fictional one is a short-term tactic that can damage your reputation and your offer.
- Be specific about the competing compensation. Vague claims ("I have other offers") are far less effective than specific ones.
- The tone should be direct and transparent, not threatening. Most experienced recruiters appreciate straightforwardness.

## Dealing with "No"

### If They Cannot Move the Numbers

Before accepting or walking away, ask one follow-up question:

> "I understand if the base is fixed. Is there any flexibility on the sign-on, equity grant, start date, or any other part of the package?"

This question converts a "no" on one component into an open question about others. Many companies have rigid base salary bands but discretion on sign-on bonuses or equity refreshers. You will not know unless you ask.

If the answer is no across the board, you have two choices.

### Accepting Without Getting the Number You Wanted

If you decide to accept despite the gap, do so gracefully and without resentment. Send a brief, professional response:

> "Thank you for looking into it. I understand the constraints, and I'm still very much looking forward to joining the team. I'm ready to move forward with the offer as presented. Please send over the paperwork when ready."

There is no benefit to expressing disappointment at this stage. You will be working with these people shortly. Close professionally.

### Walking Away

If the compensation does not meet your minimum and you have a better alternative, decline clearly and without burning the relationship:

> "I appreciate the offer and the time the team has invested in the process. Ultimately, I've decided to pursue a different opportunity that's a better fit from a compensation standpoint at this stage in my career. I have a lot of respect for [Company] and hope our paths cross again."

This close is clean. It does not invite counter-arguments, it does not apologize, and it leaves the door open. You may want to work with this recruiter or company again. Protect that relationship.
