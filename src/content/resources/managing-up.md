# Managing Up Without Sycophancy

**Target Audience:** Mid-to-senior professionals who want to increase their influence and visibility without compromising their integrity or becoming a yes-person.

---

## Mapping Your Manager's Priorities

Managing up begins with understanding what success looks like for your manager, not just for you. This is not about flattery. It is about aligning your work with what is actually important to the people who evaluate your performance.

Your manager has their own manager, their own performance metrics, and their own organizational pressures. When your work directly addresses their priorities, you become visibly valuable. When your work is excellent but unrelated to their concerns, your contributions are effectively invisible.

To map their priorities, use the following approach.

**Listen for the themes they repeat.** In every team meeting and every 1:1, notice which topics your manager returns to unprompted. The things they bring up without being asked are the things actually on their mind. Write these down for two weeks before drawing any conclusions.

**Ask a direct question in your next 1:1:** "What are the two or three things that would most improve your confidence in how the team is tracking this quarter?" This invites honesty about their actual concerns rather than their official stated goals.

**Understand their manager's priorities.** If you have the opportunity to attend a leadership all-hands or read notes from senior leadership, pay attention to what the layer above your manager is focused on. Your manager is accountable to those priorities, and work that serves them gets noticed.

Once you have this map, review your current projects against it. For anything that aligns strongly with your manager's top priorities, make that alignment explicit when you share updates: "I want to flag that this work is directly related to the latency concern you mentioned in the planning meeting."

## The "No Surprises" Rule

The single most effective thing you can do to build trust with your manager is to ensure they are never caught off guard by your work. A manager who learns about a problem from you first is in a position to help. A manager who learns about a problem from their own manager, in a meeting, without warning, is embarrassed. That embarrassment attaches to you.

The no-surprises rule means you proactively communicate anything that could affect them before they hear it from another source.

The practical threshold is simple: if there is something your manager would want to know, tell them before someone else does. This includes:

- A project running behind schedule by more than two days on a tight timeline
- A conflict with a peer or stakeholder that has not been resolved within 24 hours
- A technical problem that could affect a deadline or a user-facing feature
- An error that you discovered and corrected but that affected production, even briefly

The format does not need to be elaborate. A Slack message works:

> "Heads up before our 1:1: the deploy from yesterday morning caused a brief latency spike that lasted about 8 minutes. It is resolved now and I am writing the postmortem. Wanted you to have this before anyone asks."

That message takes 20 seconds to write and prevents your manager from being blindsided in a meeting.

## Pushing Back on Scope and Deadlines

Sycophantic managing up means agreeing to every deadline and scope request regardless of feasibility. It feels like cooperation in the short term and it reliably destroys trust over time, because commitments get missed and the pattern becomes established.

Honest managing up means flagging problems clearly and proposing solutions at the same time. Use the following scripts for common scenarios.

**Script for pushing back on an unrealistic deadline:**

> "I want to make sure we are aligned on what is achievable here. Based on the scope we have discussed, I estimate [X] days of work with the current team. If the deadline is [Y], I need us to either reduce scope, add resources, or formally accept that we are taking a risk on quality. Which of those do you want to discuss?"

**Script for pushing back on scope creep mid-project:**

> "I want to flag something before we get further in. The request for [new feature or change] is outside the original scope we planned for. I can incorporate it, but it will add [estimated time] and push the current delivery by roughly [estimate]. Should I reprioritize to accommodate it, or do you want to hold the original timeline and address this separately?"

**Script for pushing back on a decision you disagree with:**

> "I want to raise a concern before we move forward. My worry is [specific concern, with specific reason]. I understand the business pressure and I will follow your direction. I just wanted to make sure this was on your radar. If you have already considered it and decided to proceed, I am fully on board."

The last sentence is the most important part of that script. You have stated your concern and made clear you are not going to resist the decision. That is what separates a trusted advisor from a difficult report.

## Managing Incompetence

Not every manager is effective. Some are disorganized. Some are absent. Some are skilled at organizational politics but unclear on technical execution. Working for an ineffective manager is a career risk, because your output gets filtered through their lens and can be miscommunicated or underrepresented.

The goal is not to fix your manager. The goal is to protect your work, your reputation, and your development despite the structural gap.

**Create your own structure.** If your manager does not run clear 1:1s, you run them. Send an agenda the night before. If they do not track project status, you maintain a written status document and share it proactively. Operate as if the infrastructure around you is reliable, regardless of whether it is.

**Build lateral relationships.** Develop strong working relationships with peers, cross-functional partners, and the layer above your manager. Your work should be visible to more than one person. This protects you if your manager misrepresents your contributions (intentionally or not) during calibration and promotion discussions.

**Document your contributions.** The brag document process described in the self-reviews guide applies here with extra urgency. When your manager may not be accurately representing your work, your own written record is your primary asset.

**Sponsor yourself deliberately.** Find at least one person above your manager who knows your work. This does not require politicking. It means volunteering for visible cross-team projects, asking your manager's manager a thoughtful question in an all-hands, or sharing a well-written document with a broader audience. Make your work findable by people who are senior enough to advocate for you.
