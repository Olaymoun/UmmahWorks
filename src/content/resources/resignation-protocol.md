# Resignation and Handoff Protocol

Resigning from a job is a professional procedure, not an emotional event. How you leave a company directly affects your references, your reputation in your industry, and your own mental clarity going into the next role. Follow the steps in this document in order. Skipping steps creates problems that are difficult to reverse.

---

## The Resignation Meeting

Before you send any email or submit anything in writing, you must tell your manager in person (or by video call if you are remote). Managers who find out about a resignation through an email or an HR system before you told them personally will remember it. It damages trust in a way that can affect your reference.

### Prerequisites Before This Meeting

- [ ] You have a signed offer letter from your new employer in hand.
- [ ] You have confirmed your official start date and it gives you adequate time for your notice period.
- [ ] You have decided your last day. Most professionals give two weeks. Some roles with significant handoff complexity warrant four weeks, but this is your decision, not your employer's.
- [ ] You have thought through how to respond to a counter-offer, because one may come.

### The Verbal Script

Request a private meeting with your manager. Do not hint at the subject in advance. If they ask what the meeting is about, say "I have something I need to discuss with you in person."

Open the conversation directly. Do not build up to it with small talk.

"I want to be upfront with you because I respect you and our working relationship. I have decided to leave [Company Name]. I have accepted another position and my last day will be [specific date]. I wanted to tell you directly before anything official goes through HR."

Then stop talking. Let them respond. Most managers will ask where you are going and why. You are not obligated to share where you are going, but sharing the general type of opportunity is courteous: "A role in [industry/function] that is a strong fit for where I want to take my career." You do not need to explain or justify your decision.

### Handling the Counter-Offer

If your manager makes a counter-offer, thank them and do not accept on the spot regardless of your intention:

"I appreciate that. I am not in a position to make a decision about that today. Can I have some time to think about it?"

In almost every case, accepting a counter-offer is a mistake. The reasons you decided to leave do not disappear because your salary increased. Research consistently shows that the majority of employees who accept counter-offers leave the company within 12 months anyway, often under worse circumstances.

If you have already mentally committed to leaving, say so cleanly: "I am genuinely grateful, but I have made my decision. I think this is the right move for me and I want to be honest with you about that."

### What Not to Say in This Meeting

- Do not list your grievances with the company, the team, or the management.
- Do not tell your manager who on the team you think is underperforming or mismanaged.
- Do not make promises about staying longer than your stated notice period unless you have already thought through the implications.
- Do not discuss compensation at your new role unless you have a specific reason to do so.
- Do not apologize for leaving. Gratitude is appropriate. Apology implies you did something wrong.

## The Formal Notice

After your verbal conversation with your manager, send the formal resignation email to HR within the same business day. In some organizations HR will request a formal letter. This document serves as the official record of your notice period, last day, and the terms under which you are departing.

### Resignation Email Template

---

**To:** [HR contact email]
**CC:** [Your manager's email]
**Subject:** Notice of Resignation: [Your Full Name]

Dear [HR Contact Name],

I am writing to formally notify [Company Name] of my resignation, effective [your last day of employment, formatted as Month Day, Year]. This letter represents [X weeks] of advance notice from today's date.

I am grateful for the opportunities I have had during my time here and will do everything possible to ensure a smooth transition before my departure.

Please let me know what steps I need to complete during my notice period, including any offboarding documentation, exit interviews, or equipment return procedures.

Thank you,

[Your Full Name]
[Your Title]
[Your Personal Email Address]
[Your Phone Number]

---

Keep this letter brief. Do not include reasons for leaving, commentary on the company, or anything that could be read negatively if reviewed later by a future employer or in a legal context. The shorter and more neutral, the better.

## The Handoff Documentation

A clean handoff is your professional reputation walking out the door ahead of you. Colleagues will remember whether you left them with everything they needed or left gaps they had to spend weeks filling in. A well-structured transition document also protects you from post-departure calls and emails asking where to find things.

### Transition Document: Table of Contents Template

Create a document titled: **[Your Name] Transition Notes: [Date Range]**

Structure it using the following sections:

---

**1. Role Overview**
- A brief (one paragraph) description of your role and the primary responsibilities you held
- Who to contact for each major responsibility area going forward

**2. Active Projects and Status**

| Project Name | Status | Priority | Owner After Departure | Key Files/Links | Deadline |
|--------------|--------|----------|-----------------------|-----------------|----------|
| [Project A] | In progress | High | [Name/TBD] | [Link] | [Date] |
| [Project B] | Waiting on approval | Medium | [Name/TBD] | [Link] | [Date] |
| [Project C] | On hold | Low | [Name/TBD] | [Link] | None |

**3. Recurring Responsibilities**

For each recurring task, document:
- Task description and frequency
- Where the relevant files or tools live
- Step-by-step procedure (even if it seems obvious)
- Common failure modes and how to handle them

Example entry:
> **Weekly Analytics Report** (every Monday by 9am)
> - Source data in: [link to data source or folder]
> - Template in: [link to template]
> - Distribution list: [names or group alias]
> - Common issue: The export sometimes times out if the date range is over 90 days. If that happens, split into two exports and merge in the template.

**4. Key Relationships and Contacts**

| Name | Organization | Relationship | Best Way to Contact | Context |
|------|-------------|--------------|--------------------|---------| 
| [Name] | [Vendor/Partner/Team] | Our primary contact for [X] | Email preferred | [Any relevant context they should know] |

**5. Access and Credentials**

List every system, tool, account, or credential you hold that the team will need access to after you leave. Include:
- The system name
- What it is used for
- Who currently has access besides you
- How to request access or transfer ownership

Do not include actual passwords in this document. Flag access items for IT/security and complete formal transfers through the appropriate internal process.

**6. Institutional Knowledge Notes**

This is the most valuable section and the one most people skip. Write down anything that exists in your head that is not written anywhere else:
- Why a decision was made a certain way even though it looks wrong
- What a vendor does not put in their documentation but you learned through experience
- Which stakeholder is sensitive about a particular topic and why
- History and context that is not in any ticket, document, or email thread

---

Aim to have this document substantially complete by the end of your first week of notice. Share it with your manager and ask them to review it. This protects both of you and signals that you are serious about leaving well.

## The Farewell Message

Your farewell message is a professional document, not a personal blog post. It should be warm but not sentimental, brief but not cold, and forward-looking for both you and the recipients. Write two versions.

### Template 1: Farewell to Your Immediate Team

Send this to your direct teammates, people you worked with daily, and close colleagues. Can be sent via email or team chat (Slack, Teams, etc.).

---

Today is my last day at [Company Name], and I wanted to take a moment to say goodbye properly.

Working with this team has been genuinely one of the best parts of my time here. [One or two specific, genuine things you valued about the team or the work you did together: e.g., "The standard of craft we held each other to" or "The way we handled the rewrite last year, under pressure, without pointing fingers."]

I will miss this group. Please stay in touch. You can find me at:

Email: [your personal email]
LinkedIn: [your LinkedIn URL]

If there is anything I can do to help with the transition, reach out directly. I mean that.

[Your name]

---

### Template 2: Farewell to Your Broader Professional Network

Send this to your wider company network, former colleagues from other teams, and professional contacts you want to maintain but did not work with closely. Use this as a LinkedIn post or a broader distribution email.

---

Today is my last day at [Company Name] after [X years/months]. I am grateful for everything I learned here and for the people I had the opportunity to work alongside.

I am moving on to a new chapter [describe briefly if you are comfortable: e.g., "joining [Company] to work on [area]" or simply "exploring what is next"].

If we have worked together and have not connected on LinkedIn, I would love to stay in touch. And if you are ever in [your city] or looking for a conversation about [your area of expertise], reach out.

Thank you to everyone who made this experience worth it.

[Your name]
[Personal email]
[LinkedIn URL]

---

**Timing notes:**
- Send the internal team message on your last day, in the last two hours of your shift.
- Post the LinkedIn message on your last day or the day after. Do not post it before your official last day unless your company's culture explicitly supports it.
- Do not post anything that could be read as criticism of the company, even in coded form. "Ready for something more aligned with my values" is not neutral. Neither is "excited to finally work on something meaningful." Write as if your former manager will read it, because they will.
